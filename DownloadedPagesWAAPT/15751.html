Eridanus: Close enough. Ball is not a good word, though. Stars are not perfectly round. They're bit flat at the poles, the same way as our planet is. They range from small to very big! The larger they are, the faster they burn the fuel... and the faster they die. The smallest of them live millions of millions of years, while the largest live just few hundred millions of years. From what I know, our star is in the middle, and has few thousand millions of years left before it dies. Now what happens to a star when it dies depends on its size. We quite don't know what happens to the smallest, since they have yet to burn out. Our star, Sun, is larger and not as long-lived. Once it will run out of hydrogen, stuff happens. The star becomes larger and outer layers are "thrown out", presumably incinerating few of the planes. This is a red giant, and other changes happen, not sure what exactly, though. In the end, it burns out to the point it can no longer sustain itself and shrinks down to a size of planet, becoming a white dwarf. At this point, it's going on the leftovers, until it presumably burns everything and no longer emits energy and light, becoming a black dwarf. Now, the largest stars are much more interesting. Once these giants reach that point, they start fusing heavier and heavier elements... until iron, as anything further will take energy, instead of giving it. Sometime after that, the star collapses into itself, for it has no more anything to counter the force of gravity, delivering unimaginable amounts of energy, where fusion of everything heavier than iron happens. Then the star explodes, as a supernova. Usually what is left is a neutron star, which are very dense. But if the star is even more massive, it collapses even further, into a black hole. A leftover of a star which absorbs even light, provided that it's close enough.

[beat] Wow. I talked a lot, didn't it? [sheepy grin] Ehehehe! You see, astronomy is kinda of my passion...
