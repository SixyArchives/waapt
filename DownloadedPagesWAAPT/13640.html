As conceived in 1632 by [Portuguese] printing press operator Andre Fillipe, boxing was a gentleman's game. In which two men would square off and regale each other with stories monotonous for days on end, until one of them fell to the ground from boredom or exhaustion. Over the next few years, the new sport developed a respectable following of a few hundred local socialites. It was Fillipe's son -Andre Fillipe Fillipe- who developed what he called the punching strategy in 1637 after seeing a schoolboy strike another in anger, causing him to fall down. When Andre Fillipe Fillipe challenged the then champion -[British] ex-patriot "Sleepless" Andrew Bishop- to a match. Bishop was the odds on favorite. You can imagine his surprise, when he was describing what he had for breakfast that morning, Andre walked up to him and thumped him in the neck, sending him down, for the count.

While it was universally agreed that the boy had violated the spirit of the game, official were unable to find any actual rule that punching violated, and were forced to let the victory stand. His upset caused an uproar in the boxing community large enough to spill over into local newspapers, which drew the interest of many outsiders wanting to know what all the fuss was about. The newcomers were enthralled to engaged to these borderline barbaric displays of human strength and skill, and the rest is history. After a few spoil sports schoolmoms addled about safety added the padded gloves of course

Today's boxing enthusiasts fantasize about a newcomer who would rock the ring the way Andre Fillipe Fillipe did back in 1637. Calcification of the modern rules set has essentially locked the punching strategy into place, but it's easy to get caught up in the fantasy. Young scholars with big dreams often enter the ring with their crazy new trick, usually a variant of hypnosis, and though the achieve the occasional victory non of the gimmicks have been robust enough to make it to the big time.

The real wonder though, is that Andre Fillipe's original vision of boxing is still alive. Gentlman's boxing clubs can be found in cities all over the world. You can visit on most any day of the week, and see erudite gentleman exchanging pleasantries in the ring. Most people only come to watch a few hours of a match and then leave, nut every once in a while you'll find amongst your elders a stout fellow. A dire hard fan, who perhaps witnessed that historic battle between Andre Fillipe Fillipe and Sleepless Andrew Bishop, for the love of the sport must stay to witness the last glorious seconds of wakefulness slip away. Only to return again to fight another day
