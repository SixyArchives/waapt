There's really two main ways to go about making a decent character. Starting simple then add layers on layers of traits/experiences or starting complex then removing layers to show that they're not so different/are different from what everyone else believes. In both situations, it takes time and no one expects it to be absolutely amazing from the get go. Sure, it might sound good and there doesn't seem to be any harm in introducing something new, but that becomes a downfall when that one thing becomes what defines your character. You don't want to be remembered as the guy who can call on all incarnations of the Gods of the Pokeverse (disregarding how cool that may sound and how sue-ish that is), but the one that others sympathize with and try to get along with. Playing in an rp isn't really playing when your character is so over-powered that in the event your fellow players decide to actually let you stick with it, require you only show up at the climax of any arc, and not necessarily every arc either.

The traditional way, or first definition, is tested and works. It's a good method for those not truly experienced in Forum Role Plays. It allows mistakes and if necessary, gives multiple forms of development. This is kinda like working on paper with pencil. The second method is, however, a dangerous road to tread. Those not familiar with balancing both dynamic aspects and little details should not attempt this because messing up leaves a bad taste of poor execution and even worse planning. You need to give plenty of thought into every action you make because with a complex character, actions affect future actions, which in turn affect even later actions. It's the writer's job in this situation to professionally unfold this type of character. This is more like having a paper with the graphite already rubbed on and you have to erase until you have a good picture. If you mess up and try putting it back on with your pencil or whatever, it just looks sloppy.

In short, making a character doesn't have to be hard, but when you set forth to develop something without any consideration into:

A) Who you want to interact with and if it's enough chances to make your character meaningful.

B) How you want your character to be perceived by other players and if they would acknowledge him/her.

C) How long you intend to develop your character and what he/she becomes from it and any subsequent events.

D) Can you keep them interesting beyond accomplishing at least 1 long-term goal.

Then you end up with something that isn't fun for anyone. People participate in these rps for many different reasons, but the thing that keeps them here is that its interesting. Characters make the story, be it heroes or villains. Conflict creates tension. Understanding creates sympathy. Both combined makes it "real" because that is reality (or at least to the point of our willing suspension). If making a character seems a tad daunting now, remember that "simple is marvelous".
